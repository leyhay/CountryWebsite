China's history spans over 5,000 years, beginning with early settlements along the Yellow River and developing through a succession of dynasties, including the Shang, Zhou, Han, Tang, and Qing. China's dynasties include the Xia (legendary), Shang, Zhou, Qin, Han, Jin, Sui, Tang, Song, Yuan, Ming, and Qing, among many others.

The longest river in China and the third-longest river in the world, the 6,300 kilometer Yangtze River starts at the southern foothills of the snow-covered Geladandong Peak of the Tanggula Mountains in southwestern Qinghai Province. We Chinese call the Yangtze River, Chang-jiang, which means “Long River”, or just “Jiang” — river.

The yelllow river also known as the huang river is often called the cradle of Chinese civilization. With a length of 3,395 miles (5,464 km), it is the country’s second longest river—surpassed only by the Yangtze River (Chang Jiang)—and its drainage basin is the third largest in China, with an area of some 290,000 square miles (750,000 square km).

The Great Wall of China often traces the crestlines of hills and mountains as it snakes across the Chinese countryside, and about one-fourth of its length consists solely of natural barriers such as rivers and mountain ridges. It starts in the east at Shanhaiguan in Hebei Province where it meets the Bohai Sea, and stretches west to Jiayuguan Pass in Gansu Province. Primarily used as a massive defensive fortification to protect Chinese empires from invasions and raids by nomadic groups like the Xiongnu and Mongols, but also to define borders, control trade, and project imperial power over centuries.

Terracotta warriors were people who were buried with an emperor so they could protect him in the afterlife. At least 8,000 clay soilders were buried. They were assembled from separately molded parts like heads, arms, and torsos, then detailed by hand, painted with vibrant mineral pigments, and originally equipped with real bronze weapons for Emperor Qin Shi Huang's afterlife army.

Chinese new year dates back over 3,500 years to the Shang Dynasty as a ceremony honoring ancestors, evolving from ancient harvest rituals. There was a legend about scaring away a beast called Nian with red decorations and loud noises. The dragon dance was incorporated during the Tang Dynasty and red envelopes duirng the Song Dynasty. Today, it lasts 15-days marked by family gatherings, fireworks, and traditional foods, officially renamed Spring Festival in 1949.

The silk road was a vast network of ancient trade routes connecting China and Europe/Middle East from around the 2nd century BCE to the mid-15th century CE, named for China's valuable silk exports but also moving spices, ideas, religions (Buddhism, Islam), and technologies, fostering immense cultural exchange across Eurasia before being superseded by sea routes.
